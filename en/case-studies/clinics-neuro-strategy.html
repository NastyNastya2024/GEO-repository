Case: clinics

How healthcare brands become a cited source for ChatGPT/Perplexity/SGE: facts, trust, structure and local signals.

What methods are a priority and a typical strategy

Below are the steps of what they invest in GEO, AEO, AIO, SEO and AI+SMM specifically for clinics and networks, plus separately about reviews and external sites.

GEO in the context of clinics is not “abstract SEO”, but clear connection of the network to the location, so that both people and systems (maps, assistants, local search) compare the brand with real points and the area where you receive.

  • What's included: addresses and opening hours of branches, directions and entrance maps, parking/MCC, separate landing pages for a branch or city, single telephone numbers where they actually answer.
  • Service area: clearly state where you receive patients from (district, city, home visits - if available), so that requests like “near / in [city]” do not diverge from the fact.
  • Maps and directories: current cards on maps and in medical catalogs with the same NAP data (name, address, telephone) as on the website - otherwise AI and patients see two different “clinics”.
  • Link to the site: from each branch card on the website - hours, how to sign up, what services are here, a link to the profiles of doctors of this branch.

AEO — pages of services and conditions as ready-made answers to patient questions.

  • Question headings (H2) in the wording of people: “how to prepare”, “how long does it last”, “who is shown to” - with disclaimers and reservations accepted in medical marketing.
  • At the beginning of the page there is a short, clear answer, then the structure (indications, diagnosis, what to expect), without replacing the face-to-face consultation with one paragraph.
  • One main point on the URL: do not mix incompatible topics on one page for the sake of text space.

AIO — a single “fact base” about admission and conditions throughout the site.

  • Describe in the same way what is included in the reception, preparation, registration, work with insurance and payment - on the service page, in the branch FAQ and in the registration blocks.
  • Remove contradictions between sections (price/term/restrictions) so that the model does not mix different versions of the same service.

SEO — infrastructure, without which content is poorly located and indexed.

  • Technical accessibility: correct response codes, mobile version, speed, duplicates and canonical.
  • Connectivity of clusters: linking “symptom / service / doctor / branch”, so that robots and AI see a solid structure, and not scattered PDF and archived pages.

AI+SMM — when external channels copy or distort the official picture.

  • Social networks and advertising creatives: the same wording of services, promotions and restrictions as on the website; otherwise, a “blurred” or contradictory version will more often emerge in AI responses.
  • Record aggregators and catalogues: checking the list of services, prices (if you publish), graphics - with official pages.

How important are reviews and external sources - and where should they be?

  • For what: reviews and third-party mentions are a signal of trust for people and additional context for models (who you are, how you are assessed, not just your texts). By themselves, they do not replace high-quality answer pages on the site.
  • Where to post and support: cards (review block on the organization’s card), clinic profiles on medical aggregators and recording services, specialized catalogs (with a single NAP), official social networks with responses to comments and current contacts for the recording.
  • Website as canon: on the domain - review policy (if accepted), links “how to leave a review” on allowed sites, but not duplicating the full text of all reviews instead of the unique content of service pages.
  • External media and partners: articles, interviews, directories - useful if the facts (name, address, specialization) coincide with the site; discrepancies reduce trust in both search and AI responses.
  • Working with reviews: clinic responses on maps and in aggregators in a neutral tone, without promises of treatment in correspondence and with reference to an appointment/face-to-face appointment - this is part of the single “face” of the brand.

Typical strategy: coordinate NAP and cards by points → pages of departments and doctors → thematic pages-answers to information demand → FAQ on registration and payment. The general order of methods is in review of promotion methods; see also local business.

Which “well-known” examples to focus on (non-Russian brands)

  • Mayo Clinic: encyclopedic nature, clear definitions, building blocks and medical disclaimers.
  • Cleveland Clinic: taxonomy of topics, internal links and “answer-first” structure.
  • Kaiser Permanente: local availability, appointments/insurance, department pages and doctor profiles.

What AI “sees” and why it is important for clinics to be accurate

Models prefer sources that contain verifiable facts: definitions, criteria, contraindications, authorship, update dates, links to studies, as well as stable entities (brand, doctors, departments, addresses, licenses).

Which IT products are “better” for clinics for AI: a website or just maps?

This is not about replacing one thing with another, but about different signal depths. For questions like “where are you, how to sign up, opening hours,” maps and aggregators are often sufficient. For the questions “what is the procedure, who is shown, how to prepare, what is included in the appointment,” models are looking for expanded pages with explicit structure - they are easiest to give to clinic website (or on a separate medical portal of the network under your control).

  • Maps and directories (Google/Yandex, etc.) — strengths: address, phone number, hours, reviews, photos, “clinic” category. For AI, this is a signal of “place essence” and local relevance. Weakness: there is little space for criteria for indications, disclaimers and links to the evidence base - it is almost impossible to collect answers about the essence of treatment from there.
  • Official website (public HTML pages) - strengths: services, doctors, branches, FAQ, articles with author and date, “when to contact” tables, policies - things from which it is convenient to extract quotes. This is the main channel for AEO/AIO; Without it, the clinic often remains in the shadow of more detailed competitors in answers about “how it works for you.”
  • Record aggregators and medical directories - useful as second layer: NAP confirmation, list of services, sometimes prices. For AI, it is important that the text does not contradict the site; otherwise, the model “throws up its hands” or averages conflicting numbers.
  • Personal account, mobile application, internal CRM - as a rule are not indexed and are not included in the open ChatGPT/search response. This is about patient service, and not about the body of knowledge for neural networks.
  • Practical conclusion: minimal outline for local demand - neat cards on cards + uniform contacts. For the demand “tell us about the service / compare options” without public response pages on the domain (or a controlled medical portal) is almost impossible to do.

AI-friendly page structure for quoting

  1. Short answer (TL;DR) at the beginning + 3–5 bulletins with key points.
  2. Symptoms/indications → diagnosis → treatment in the same order on all pages of the topic.
  3. Criteria table (when to contact, “red flags”, timing, cost/insurance).
  4. FAQ with direct questions (“How long does it last...”, “Who shouldn’t...”, “How to prepare...”).

Trust (E‑E‑A‑T) for clinics: what to add

  • Author/Editor (doctor/medical editor), qualifications, regalia, profile.
  • Update date and “what has changed.”
  • Sources (guidelines, publications), neat quotes.
  • Branch pages: address, hours, services, equipment, recording.

Technical signals (AEO/GEO)

  • Schema.org: Organization/LocalBusiness, MedicalOrganization, Physician, FAQPage, Article.
  • Local data: NAP consistency, maps, reviews, photo content, UTM tags.
  • Internal linking: “condition → service → doctor → branch.”

Mini-implementation plan for 30 days

  1. Collect 30–50 key questions from patients and organize them into topics.
  2. Rewrite 10 “money” pages into an answer-first structure + criteria tables.
  3. Add authorship, dates, sources and FAQ markup.
  4. Update branch/doctor profiles and local pages.
